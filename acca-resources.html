The Facts

What the data actually shows.

Prohibition-era talking points don't hold up to scrutiny. Here's what peer-reviewed science and federal data tell us.

30yr
Low in Teen Cannabis Use

Teen cannabis use is at a 30-year low. Between 2012 and 2025, past-year use fell 30% among 12th graders, 44% among 10th graders, and 34% among 8th graders. Legalization has not caused teen use to rise — in 19 of 21 states with pre/post data, it decreased after legalization.

Source: NIDA Monitoring the Future Survey, 2024

88%
Of Americans Support Legal Cannabis

Nearly 9 in 10 U.S. adults say cannabis should be legal for medical or recreational use. Just 11% say it should not be legal at all. This is not a fringe position — it is mainstream American opinion across party lines, races, and age groups.

Source: Pew Research Center, March 2024

$20B+
In State Tax Revenue Generated

Legal adult-use cannabis sales generated more than $20 billion in state tax revenue by Q1 2024. That money funds schools, public health programs, and community reinvestment — resources that prohibition never delivered.

Source: Marijuana Policy Project, 2024

Myth vs. Fact

Common claims. Real answers.

Myth

Cannabis edibles are a top cause of childhood poisoning

Proponents of restrictive packaging bills often cite rising ER visits as evidence of a crisis caused by legal edibles.

Fact

Edibles don't crack the top 10 causes in children ages 0–5

Oregon Poison Center 2023 data shows household cleaners, cosmetics, and pain relievers each cause over 1,600 pediatric incidents annually. Cannabis edibles account for far fewer. Rising ER numbers largely reflect parents feeling safer disclosing post-legalization — not a true spike in incidents.

→ Oregon Poison Center Annual Report 2023
Myth

THC can cause fatal respiratory depression like opioids

Horror stories about children on ventilators have been used to justify sweeping restrictions on legal cannabis products.

Fact

THC does not cause respiratory depression — this is peer-reviewed science

Unlike opioids, THC receptors in the brainstem are only presynaptic — they cannot directly shut down breathing. Severe cases in children are likely pharmacological outliers involving undiagnosed conditions, extreme dose-to-weight ratios, or adulterated unregulated products.

→ Wiese et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2023
Myth

Legalization increases teen drug use

One of the most persistent arguments against cannabis reform is that it sends a permissive message to young people.

Fact

Teen use is at a 30-year low in legalized states

Federal survey data shows the opposite of what prohibition advocates predict. When cannabis is regulated, it's harder for teens to access — dealers don't card. The regulated market creates age verification that the black market never had.

→ NIDA Monitoring the Future Survey, 2024
Myth

Individual unit packaging protects children from edibles

Legislation like Oregon's SB 1548 assumed that individually wrapping every gummy unit would prevent pediatric exposure.

Fact

Education and safe storage are what actually change outcomes

88% of pediatric cannabis edible ER events are attributed to lack of safe storage — not product design. CPSC analysis found it cannot conclude that individual unit packaging outperforms standard child-resistant packaging. Oregon's own Poison Center director, Dr. Rob Hendrickson, publicly stated the real solution is education and locked storage.

→ Dr. Hendrickson, OPB Think Out Loud, 2025

How Regulation Works

The licensed market is the safe market.

Every licensed cannabis product in a legal state goes through a rigorous chain of oversight that the unregulated market — including homemade edibles — will never match.

1

Cultivation

Licensed grows with state inspections, pesticide restrictions, and documented inputs

2

Lab Testing

Mandatory third-party testing for potency, pesticides, heavy metals, and microbials

3

Labeling

Required dosage information, ingredients, warnings, and child-resistant packaging

4

Tracking

Seed-to-sale tracking systems that monitor every product from harvest to point of sale

5

Age Verification

ID required at point of sale — 21+ only. No licensed retailer can sell to a minor legally

✓ Licensed Legal Market

  • Mandatory third-party lab testing for potency and contaminants
  • Child-resistant packaging required by law
  • Accurate dosage labeling on every product
  • Age verification at point of sale — 21+ only
  • State inspections and license requirements
  • Seed-to-sale tracking for full accountability
  • No synthetic cannabinoids or adulterants
  • Consumer recourse if products are mislabeled

✕ Unregulated / Black Market

  • No testing — unknown potency, pesticides, or adulterants
  • No packaging requirements — often stored in unmarked containers
  • No dosage information — consumers guessing at every use
  • No age verification — sold to anyone
  • No oversight or inspections of any kind
  • No accountability chain — no way to trace a bad product
  • Synthetic cannabinoids increasingly common — 80x more potent than THC
  • No consumer recourse whatsoever

Know Your Rights

Informed consumers are protected consumers.

Your rights as a cannabis consumer vary by state, but knowing the basics means you're never caught off guard.

🏠

Home Cultivation

Many legal states allow adults to grow a limited number of cannabis plants at home for personal use. Oregon allows up to 4 plants per household for adults 21+. Know your state's limit — and keep them out of public view.

State-by-state laws →
🚗

Transportation & Travel

In legal states, you can transport cannabis in a sealed container in your vehicle — but open containers follow the same rules as open alcohol. Never cross state lines with cannabis, even between two legal states. Federal jurisdiction applies on interstate roads.

Transportation rules →
💼

Workplace Protections

Workplace protections for cannabis consumers are still evolving. Some states now prohibit employers from discriminating against off-duty cannabis use, but many do not. Safety-sensitive positions and federal contractors have fewer protections. Know your state's law before disclosing use to an employer.

Workplace rights →
🏥

Medical Patient Rights

Medical cannabis patients often have additional protections — higher possession limits, access to dispensaries in states without adult-use, and in some cases employment and housing protections. A valid medical card can also provide legal protection in states with decriminalization but not full legalization.

Patient resources →
✊

If You Are Cited or Arrested

Even in legal states, violations can occur — exceeding possession limits, consuming in public, or driving impaired. If cited or arrested: stay calm, don't argue at the scene, don't consent to searches without a warrant, and contact a cannabis-knowledgeable attorney immediately.

Find legal help →
🗳️

Your Right to Advocate

The most powerful right you have is your voice. You can testify at legislative hearings, write letters to the editor, contact your representatives, and vote. Consumer voices in the room when bad policy is being made change outcomes — as ACCA proved when Oregon's SB 1548 was defeated.

Policy Watch

Bills that affect you.

We track legislation that impacts cannabis consumers in Oregon, Hawai'i, and nationally. When something moves, you'll know.

Defeated — Oregon 2025

SB 1548 — Individual Edible Packaging & 10mg Unit Cap

Oregon Senate Bill 1548 would have required individual wrapping for every cannabis edible unit regardless of THC content per unit, and would have limited each unit to no more than 10mg THC. The bill was backed by claims of a child safety crisis — claims that were not supported by Oregon's own Poison Center data, peer-reviewed science on THC pharmacology, or evidence on the effectiveness of unit packaging. ACCA organized consumer testimony, distributed research, and helped make the consumer voice heard in the House. The bill was defeated.

Outcome: Defeated in the Oregon House, 2025 session. Expected to return in 2027.

Safe Storage

The real solution is in your hands.

The data is clear: the vast majority of pediatric cannabis exposure events come down to one thing — adults not storing products securely. This is a fixable problem.

88%
Of pediatric cannabis edible ER events attributed to lack of safe storage
Canadian Review, ScienceDirect
45%
Of cannabis users in homes with children received zero storage information from a retailer or clinician
California Pediatric ED Survey, WestJEM 2021
91%
Of pediatric edible exposures occur in the child's own home — where an adult brought the product
AAP Pediatrics, 2023
🔒

Lock It Up

Store all cannabis products in a locked container or cabinet. Children are resourceful — a high shelf is not enough.

📦

Keep Original Packaging

Licensed cannabis comes in child-resistant packaging. Don't transfer products to unlabeled containers — the packaging is part of the safety system.

🗣️

Talk to Your Household

Everyone in the home should know where cannabis is stored, why it's locked away, and that it's not food — even if it looks like it.

📱

Know Poison Control

Save the number: 1-800-222-1222. If a child ingests cannabis, call immediately. Most cases are manageable with prompt attention.

THC Pharmacology

The Endocannabinoid System and Breathing

Wiese et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience, April 2023

Comprehensive peer-reviewed literature review from the University of Arizona Department of Pharmacology. Explicitly states THC does not cause respiratory depression. CB1 receptors are only presynaptic — unlike opioid receptors — meaning they cannot cause fatal respiratory arrest. Distinguishes natural THC from synthetic cannabinoids, which are 80x more potent and linked to mass casualty events.

Pediatric Safety

Oregon Poison Center Annual Report

Oregon Health & Science University, 2023

Cannabis edibles do not appear in the top 10 causes of accidental poisoning in children ages 0–5 in Oregon. Household cleaners (1,659 incidents), cosmetics (1,636), and analgesics (1,606) all rank far higher. Provides critical baseline for evaluating the proportionality of restrictive cannabis packaging legislation.

Safe Storage

Pediatric Cannabis Edible Exposures — California ED Survey

Western Journal of Emergency Medicine (WestJEM), PMC, 2021

Survey of cannabis users in homes with children at a California pediatric emergency department. Found 45% received zero storage information from a retailer or clinician. Only 44.8% kept cannabis both locked and hidden. Confirms that the storage education gap — not product design — is the primary intervention point.
